### The Salafī position

The Salafī school holds that any religious occasion or observance unknown to the Prophet ﷺ, the Companions, and the first three generations is a *bidʿah* in the technical sense — an innovation in the religion — and that the Prophet's ﷺ statement *kullu bidʿatin ḍalālah* ("every innovation is misguidance") admits no exceptions. On this reading, the Mawlid — which began centuries after the Salaf — is a prohibited innovation regardless of the otherwise lawful acts that take place during it. The Salafī school further argues that loving the Prophet ﷺ is best expressed by following his Sunnah throughout the year rather than by celebrating his birthday on a single date.

### The classical Sunnī position

The classical Sunnī position rests on a categorical distinction internal to *uṣūl al-fiqh*: between *bidʿah ḍalāla* (a religious innovation that contradicts established Sharīʿah) and *bidʿah ḥasana* (a new occasion or arrangement that gathers people around things already legislated — Qurʾān recitation, ṣalawāt on the Prophet ﷺ, charity, narrating his life). This distinction goes back at least to ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb's well-known reaction to the congregational tarāwīḥ ("*niʿmati l-bidʿatu hādhihi*") and was elaborated by Imam al-Shāfiʿī. Imam Jalāl al-Dīn al-Suyūṭī, in *Ḥusn al-Maqṣid fī ʿAmal al-Mawlid*, classes the Mawlid as a *bidʿah ḥasana* under this typology. Ibn Ḥajar al-ʿAsqalānī (the muḥaddith of Egypt) and Ibn Ḥajar al-Haytamī both endorsed the same classification. The dominant fatwa of all four madhāhib historically follows them — though specific contemporary mufti institutions (notably Deobandī Ḥanafī ones) have arrived at the Salafī conclusion via a different route.

### Where the Salafī position runs into difficulty

The Salafī rejection of the Mawlid rests on the maxim *kullu bidʿatin ḍalālah* read absolutely. The first difficulty: the same Companion ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb (raḍiya Allāhu ʿanhu), whom the Salafī tradition reveres, used the word *bidʿa* approvingly when he instituted congregational tarāwīḥ — *niʿmati l-bidʿatu hādhihi* (Bukhārī). The Salaf's own usage qualifies the maxim, and the Salafī position has not produced an exegesis of the maxim that accommodates ʿUmar's usage.

The second difficulty is consistency. The Salafī tradition accepts as legitimate many institutions unknown to the Prophet ﷺ and the first three generations: the canonical compilation of ḥadīth into *Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī* (~250 AH), the codified printing of the Qurʾān in modern muṣḥaf form, the institutional Friday khuṭbah format used in Salafī mosques today, the use of microphones for the adhān, the global Saudi-funded religious-broadcasting infrastructure. None of these has explicit Prophetic sanction. No principled criterion has been articulated that places the Mawlid on the prohibited side of the line and these other innovations on the permitted side. The classical Sunnī *uṣūl al-fiqh* distinction between *bidʿa ḍalāla* and *bidʿa ḥasana*, traceable to Imam al-Shāfiʿī and elaborated by al-Nawawī, gives such a criterion; the Salafī position has not produced one to replace it.
