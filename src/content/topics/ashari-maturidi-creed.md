### The Salafī position

The Salafī school accepts that Imam Abū al-Ḥasan al-Ashʿarī (d. 324 AH) is a major historical figure and that he ended his life on the Atharī (text-based) creed of Imam Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal — citing his late work *al-Ibāna ʿan Uṣūl al-Diyāna*. What it rejects is the kalām-based methodology that subsequent Ashʿarī and Māturīdī scholars (al-Bāqillānī, al-Juwaynī, al-Ghazālī, al-Rāzī, al-Taftāzānī, al-Jurjānī) developed. On the Salafī reading, that methodology — using the conceptual vocabulary of the philosophers and *mutakallimūn* to defend the divine attributes — introduced ta'wīl, *tafwīḍ al-maʿnā*, and other interpretive techniques that the Salaf did not employ, and produced positions on the divine attributes (especially on direction, place, and the literal/non-literal status of "hand," "face," "istiwāʾ," "nuzūl") that depart from the apparent reading of the texts.

### The classical Sunnī position

The classical Sunnī position is that the way of the Salaf was a creedal *stance* — affirming what Allāh affirms of Himself, denying anthropomorphism, refusing the speculative excesses of philosophers and Muʿtazila — rather than a single methodology written down in advance. That stance is what the Ashʿarī and Māturīdī schools systematized, in the language and against the challenges of their own time. The chain of transmission is not in dispute: from al-Ashʿarī and al-Māturīdī through al-Bāqillānī, al-Juwaynī, al-Ghazālī, al-Rāzī, al-Āmidī, al-Bayḍāwī, al-Ījī, al-Taftāzānī, al-Jurjānī, al-Sanūsī — every one of them an established Sunnī authority taught alongside the four madhāhib's fiqh manuals at al-Azhar, in the Ḥaramayn before the modern era, in the Ottoman institutions, in Mauritanian and Yemeni traditional schools, and in Deobandī and Barelvī curricula in South Asia. To call these two schools deviant is to call the four-century mainline of formally-trained Sunnī scholarship deviant.

### Where the Salafī position runs into difficulty

The first difficulty: the Salafī claim that the Ashʿarī and Māturīdī schools depart from the Salaf rests on a contestable account of what the Salaf themselves affirmed. Imam Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal's documented practice on the mutashābih verses was *imrāruhā kamā jāʾat* (passing them by as they came) — reciting them, affirming them as revealed, and declining to commit to any specific meaning. This is *tafwīḍ al-maʿnā* (consigning the meaning to Allāh's knowledge). It is not the same as the modern Salafī position, drawn from Ibn Taymiyya, that affirms the apparent linguistic meaning *bilā kayf*. *Tafwīḍ* says: I do not know what this means. *Bilā kayf* affirmation says: I know the meaning (the apparent one); I just do not know the modality. These are distinct positions, routinely conflated in modern Salafī polemic but distinguishable in the source texts.

The second difficulty is the use of *al-Ibāna ʿan Uṣūl al-Diyāna* as evidence that al-Ashʿarī "returned to" the Atharī creed at the end of his life. Whatever the merits of that historical claim about al-Ashʿarī's biography, *al-Ibāna* itself uses *kalām*-style argumentation extensively — the very methodology Salafī polemic rejects. A document held up as proof of return-from-kalām that itself deploys kalām is not the simple textual victory the citation implies.

The third difficulty is the broader inconsistency on *kalām*. The Salafī tradition rejects kalām in principle but uses kalām techniques in practice — when refuting the Muʿtazila on the createdness of the Qurʾān, when refuting the Twelver Shīʿa on the imamate, when refuting the philosophers on cosmology. Ibn Taymiyya's *Darʾ Taʿāruḍ al-ʿAql wa-l-Naql* and Ibn al-Qayyim's *al-Ṣawāʿiq al-Mursala* are themselves substantial exercises in kalām. A principle held in theory but suspended in practice when needed is internally inconsistent.
