### The Salafī position

The Salafī position on tarāwīḥ rakaʿāt takes ʿĀʾisha's narration in *Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī* and *Muslim* as decisive: the Prophet ﷺ "did not pray more than eleven rakaʿāt in Ramaḍān or at other times." Al-Albānī's treatise *Ṣalāt al-Tarāwīḥ* argues on this basis that praying beyond 8 or 11 rakaʿāt lacks a prophetic precedent and that the widespread practice of 20 is an ungrounded accretion. A current of contemporary Saudi-Salafī opinion follows this reading, and some of its adherents — as Shaykh Ibn ʿUthaymīn himself noted in *al-Sharḥ al-Mumtiʿ* — "aggressively denounce those who do more, saying that they are sinners." The methodological move is direct: the Prophet's ﷺ documented practice sets the ceiling, and any numerical addition to it lacks textual warrant.

### The classical Sunnī position

The classical Sunnī position, uniform across all four madhāhib, is that 20 rakaʿāt is the established Sunnah of the Muslim community, instituted by ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb with Companion consensus (*ijmāʿ ṣaḥābī*) in the early years after the Prophet's ﷺ death. The transmitted evidence is explicit: al-Bayhaqī records in *al-Sunan al-Kubrā* the report of al-Sāʾib ibn Yazīd that "in the time of ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb they prayed twenty rakaʿāt in Ramaḍān." Ibn Abī Shaybah records the same in *al-Muṣannaf*. Al-Sarkhasi states in *al-Mabsūṭ* (2/145): "It is twenty rakaʿāt, apart from witr, in our view." Ibn Qudāmah confirms in *al-Mughnī* (1/457) that this was also the position of Imams al-Thawrī, Abū Ḥanīfa, and al-Shāfiʿī. Imam al-Nawawī summarises the Shāfiʿī position in *al-Majmūʿ* (4/31): "Tarāwīḥ prayer is Sunnah according to scholarly consensus. Our view is that it is twenty rakaʿāt." The Mālikīs hold 36 in the Madinan tradition. Not one of the four madhāhib prescribes fewer than 20.

### Where the Salafī position runs into difficulty

The central difficulty is an internal inconsistency that the Salafī position has not resolved. ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb instituted the congregational tarāwīḥ that every mosque in the Salafī world prays. He did not institute the prayer and separately decree a rakaʿāt count: he instituted 20 rakaʿāt in congregation, and the Companions who prayed behind him — the very Ṣaḥāba the Salafī tradition treats as the highest authority after the Prophet ﷺ — prayed 20 without recorded objection. The Salafī position accepts the congregational form (otherwise there would be no tarāwīḥ in mosques at all) but rejects the count that came with it. The principle by which the form is accepted and the count is rejected has not been textually established.

The second difficulty is evidentiary. The argument that ʿĀʾisha's narration sets an 8 or 11 rakaʿāt ceiling for tarāwīḥ requires reading a descriptive narration as a prescriptive maximum. Imam Ibn Taymiyya — whose authority the Salafī tradition treats as primary — explicitly rejected this reading: "If a person prays Tarāwīḥ according to the madhhabs of Abū Ḥanīfa, al-Shāfiʿī and Aḥmad, with twenty rakaʿāt, or according to the madhhab of Mālik, with thirty-six rakaʿāt, or with thirteen or eleven rakaʿāt, he has done well, as Imam Aḥmad said, because there is nothing to specify the number." (*al-Ikhtiyārāt*, p. 64) A position that cites Ibn Taymiyya as its principal authority but reaches a conclusion he explicitly rejected is in tension with itself.

The third difficulty is the nature of the Companion practice. Al-Bayhaqī's report from al-Sāʾib ibn Yazīd is not a marginal transmission: it is transmitted in the *Sunan al-Kubrā*, the *Muṣannaf* of Ibn Abī Shaybah, and cited without challenge by Ibn Qudāmah in *al-Mughnī* as the established Companion practice. A methodological framework that holds Companion consensus to be authoritative but declines to follow it when it contradicts a preferred reading of a single ḥadīth applies its principles selectively.
