### The Salafī position

The Salafī school accepts individual dhikr loudly and dhikr after the obligatory prayer (the latter being established from a Bukhārī/Muslim ḥadīth). What it rejects is *fixed* congregational gatherings convened *for* dhikr — circles in which a specific waẓīfa is recited collectively at a set time. The argument: there is no Prophetic command to convene such gatherings, and inventing a fixed religious occasion that the Prophet ﷺ and the Companions did not establish falls under the general principle that every innovation is misguidance. Loud collective dhikr is further argued to disturb worshippers in ṣalāh, to be liable to ostentation (*riyāʾ*), and to substitute communal performance for the inward private dhikr the Qurʾān commends.

### The classical Sunnī position

The classical Sunnī position, set out most authoritatively in Imam al-Nawawī's *al-Adhkār* and *Sharḥ Muslim*,[^nawawi-adhkar] permits group dhikr — including loud collective recitation in masjids — provided it does not disturb worshippers in ṣalāh. The textual basis is multiple: the ḥadīth of Ibn ʿAbbās in Bukhārī and Muslim that the Companions used to know the prayer had ended by the loud dhikr afterwards;[^ibn-abbas-dhikr] the ḥadīth of the angels surrounding gatherings of dhikr; and the general Qurʾānic command to remember Allāh "abundantly." The Ḥanafī, Mālikī, and Shāfiʿī schools all permit group dhikr historically; the Ḥanbalī school permits it with the disturbance condition. Sufi turuq across all four madhāhib have built their devotional practice around it for over a millennium.

### Where the Salafī position runs into difficulty

The Salafī rejection rests on the principle that acts of worship are *tawqīfī* — they require explicit textual sanction. The first difficulty: the principle is being applied selectively. The Salafī tradition accepts a long list of *substantive* post-Prophetic additions to acts of worship — additions instituted not by the Prophet ﷺ but by the Companions and Tabiʿīn themselves, in restriction or modification of prescribed rites — as legitimate:

- **The compilation of the Qurʾān into a single muṣḥaf,** undertaken by Abū Bakr (raḍiya Llāhu ʿanhu) on ʿUmar's advice and standardised under ʿUthmān (raḍiya Llāhu ʿanhu) — Zayd ibn Thābit (raḍiya Llāhu ʿanhu) himself initially objected: "How can you do something the Messenger of Allāh ﷺ did not do?"
- **The Islamic Hijrī calendar,** instituted by ʿUmar (raḍiya Llāhu ʿanhu) — a calendrical system not present in the time of the Prophet ﷺ, governing the timing of every obligatory act of worship (Ramaḍān, ḥajj, the zakāt year).
- **Tarāwīḥ as twenty rakaʿāt in congregation behind one imām,** instituted by ʿUmar — the same Companion who used the word *bidʿa* approvingly when he did so ("*niʿmati l-bidʿatu hādhihi*"). Al-Bayhaqī records in *al-Sunan al-Kubrā* via al-Sāʾib ibn Yazīd: "In the time of ʿUmar they would pray twenty rakaʿāt in the month of Ramaḍān; in the time of ʿUthmān they would lean on their walking sticks because of the difficulty of the prayer." The *Muṣannaf* of Ibn Abī Shaybah records the same practice under ʿUmar, ʿAlī, ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn al-Aswad, and ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz. Prayed today in the Ḥaramayn under Salafī authority — the very form of "added bidʿah" the Salafī objection should reject.
- **The first adhān of Jumuʿah,** instituted by ʿUthmān (raḍiya Llāhu ʿanhu) — recorded in *Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī*. Ibn Ḥajar al-ʿAsqalānī in *Fatḥ al-Bārī*: "ʿUthmān introduced the adhān to inform the people that the time for prayer has come in, in analogy to the rest of the prayer… and in this is a deduction from the basis [of the Sharīʿah] and does not violate it." A second adhān not present in the time of the Prophet ﷺ, now universal in Salafī mosques.
- **Bilāl's (raḍiya Llāhu ʿanhu) restriction of nafl prayer to every wuḍūʾ** — a self-imposed restriction of an unrestricted act of worship. The Prophet ﷺ commended rather than condemned it (*Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī*, *Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim*); Ibn Ḥajar concludes the ḥadīth shows "the permissibility of *ijtihād* in setting a specific time for acts of worship." This is precisely the methodology the Salafī objection rejects when applied to dhikr.
- **The "*al-ṣalātu khayrun min al-nawm*" addition to the Fajr adhān,** introduced by Bilāl himself (Ibn Mājah, Dārimī) — a permanent addition to the prescribed adhān, universal in Salafī Saudi mosques today.
- **Companion additions to the Prophetic talbiya** — ʿUmar, ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿUmar, Ibn Masʿūd, Abū Hurayrah, Jābir, and Ibn ʿAbbās all added wording to the Prophet's ﷺ talbiya. *Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim* records ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿUmar's: *Labbayka Labbayka wa Saʿdayka wal-Khayru bi-yadayka Labbayka wal-raghbāʾu ilayka wal-ʿamal*. An explicit addition to one of the inviolable rites of Ḥajj, accepted across the four madhāhib.
- **Cash zakāt al-fiṭr,** practiced by the Tābiʿīn (most prominently among the Ḥanafī chain following ʿUmar ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz) — a substitution of the Prophet's ﷺ explicit specification of a *ṣāʿ* of staple food with monetary equivalent. Now the dominant practice across the Muslim world, including in many Salafī jurisdictions.
- **Congregational duʿāʾ after the obligatory prayer; the *khatm al-Qurʾān* duʿāʾ in tarāwīḥ; specifying days for religious storytelling and study circles; the personal litanies of the Companions themselves** (collected in the *Muṣannafs* of ʿAbd al-Razzāq and Ibn Abī Shaybah) — multiple post-Prophetic devotional restrictions accepted as legitimate across the Salafī tradition without explicit Prophetic command.

These are not microphones and loudspeakers (utilities that don't change the form of worship); they are *substantive* additions to and modifications of the prescribed rites — instituted by the Salaf themselves and accepted by Salafī fiqh as legitimate. The principle "no addition to worship without explicit Prophetic command" is therefore not the principle the Salafī tradition actually applies. What it applies in practice is closer to the classical Sunnī *uṣūl al-fiqh* distinction: *tawqīfī* governs the core prescribed rites (the five prayers, the prescribed fasts, the ḥajj's defined arkān), while occasions for already-permitted devotion (dhikr, recitation, ṣalawāt, supererogatory prayer, duʿāʾ) admit additions connected by analogy or principle to a textually-established root. The Salafī rejection of group dhikr — but acceptance of every item above — has not produced a principled criterion that puts group dhikr on the prohibited side and ʿUmar's twenty-rakaʿāt tarāwīḥ on the permitted side.

The second difficulty is direct textual evidence. The ḥadīth of Ibn ʿAbbās in *Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī* and *Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim* — that the Companions used to know the Prophet's ﷺ prayer had ended by the loud dhikr afterwards — explicitly affirms collective audible dhikr in the masjid. This is the very form of evidence the Salafī methodology requires for permissibility, present in the strongest of authentic chains, and the Salafī treatments tend to qualify the ḥadīth's wording in ways the wording itself does not invite. Where a Companion's practice has been questioned, classical scholars note that Imam al-Bazzāzī's reading of the Ibn Masʿūd reports is that any objection was to treating group dhikr as a *binding* obligation — not to the practice itself under the general Qurʾānic command.[^bazzazi]

Sh. ʿAbd al-Fattāḥ al-Yāfiʿī's contemporary work on bidʿah collects most of the above examples, with their classical attestations, in a single sustained argument. Sh. Yaḥyā Rhodus (al-Maqāṣid Seminary, Cambridge / SeekersGuidance) and Sh. Dr. Ṣalāḥ Abū al-Ḥāj (Dean of Ḥanafī Fiqh, World Islamic Sciences and Education University, Amman) — both recognised contemporary Sunnī scholars whose published Arabic and English work treats this question — affirm the same classical Sunnī *uṣūl* framework: the *tawqīfī* principle governs prescribed rites; restricted additions to unrestricted acts of devotion fall under general permissibility when connected to a Sharīʿah-recognised principle.

Al-Nawawī's *al-Adhkār* is the primary classical reference for the permissibility of group dhikr across the madhāhib.

[^ibn-abbas-dhikr]: Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, no. 841 (Kitāb al-Adhān, "Bāb al-dhikr baʿda al-ṣalāh"); Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim, no. 583 (Kitāb al-Masājid). Ibn ʿAbbās (raḍiya Llāhu ʿanhumā) narrates: "I used to know when the prayer ended by the takbīr." Imam al-Jazarī and Imam al-Suyūṭī both cite this ḥadīth as evidence for the permissibility of audible group dhikr in the masjid after the prayer.

[^bazzazi]: Ḥāfiẓ al-Dīn al-Bazzāzī (d. 827/1424), *Fatāwā al-Bazzāziyya* (on the margin of *al-Fatāwā al-Hindiyya*), where he distinguishes between dhikr in gatherings as a permitted and praiseworthy act under the general Qurʾānic command and the blameworthy innovation of treating such gatherings as an independently obligatory rite (*wājib* or *sunna muʾakkada*). The distinction preserves group dhikr as permissible while addressing the concern that elevating it to obligation-status would be an addition to the sharīʿah.
