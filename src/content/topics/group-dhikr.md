### The Salafī position

The Salafī school accepts individual dhikr loudly and dhikr after the obligatory prayer (the latter being established from a Bukhārī/Muslim ḥadīth). What it rejects is *fixed* congregational gatherings convened *for* dhikr — circles in which a specific waẓīfa is recited collectively at a set time. The argument: there is no Prophetic command to convene such gatherings, and inventing a fixed religious occasion that the Prophet ﷺ and the Companions did not establish falls under the general principle that every innovation is misguidance. Loud collective dhikr is further argued to disturb worshippers in ṣalāh, to be liable to ostentation (*riyāʾ*), and to substitute communal performance for the inward private dhikr the Qurʾān commends.

### The classical Sunnī position

The classical Sunnī position, set out most authoritatively in Imam al-Nawawī's *al-Adhkār* and *Sharḥ Muslim*, permits group dhikr — including loud collective recitation in masjids — provided it does not disturb worshippers in ṣalāh. The textual basis is multiple: the ḥadīth of Ibn ʿAbbās in Bukhārī and Muslim that the Companions used to know the prayer had ended by the loud dhikr afterwards; the ḥadīth of the angels surrounding gatherings of dhikr; and the general Qurʾānic command to remember Allāh "abundantly." The Ḥanafī, Mālikī, and Shāfiʿī schools all permit group dhikr historically; the Ḥanbalī school permits it with the disturbance condition. Sufi turuq across all four madhāhib have built their devotional practice around it for over a millennium.

### Where the disagreement actually sits

The two sides agree on the foundational texts — the same ḥadīth on the angels and the same Qurʾānic verses on dhikr. They disagree on a single interpretive question: does the absence of an explicit Prophetic command to convene a fixed dhikr gathering mean such gatherings are prohibited, or does it simply mean the *form* of the gathering was not legislated and therefore falls under the general permissibility of dhikr in any lawful form? The Salafī school takes the first reading via the principle that worship requires a specific textual sanction (*tawqīfī*); the classical school applies that principle to specifically defined acts of worship (the prescribed prayers, fasts, etc.) but not to occasions for already-permitted devotion.
