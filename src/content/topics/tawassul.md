### The Salafī position

The Salafī school accepts three forms of tawassul as textually established: through Allāh's beautiful names and attributes (Q 7:180, "*wa-li-Llāhi al-asmāʾu al-ḥusnā fa-dʿūhu bihā*"), through a Muslim's own righteous deeds (the ḥadīth of the three men trapped in the cave who asked Allāh by their good actions), and through a living righteous person's duʿāʾ on one's behalf. Tawassul through the rank or honour of the deceased Prophet ﷺ or a righteous person who has died is rejected as an innovation without textual basis; it is held to risk *shirk akbar* (major shirk) when the speaker addresses the deceased, and at minimum to be a *dharīʿah* (means leading to shirk) that must be blocked under the principle of *sadd al-dharāʾiʿ*. Istighātha — calling on someone for help — addressed to a non-living person is generally classed as shirk in worship, since asking is itself a form of worship and worship is owed to Allāh alone.

### The classical Sunnī position

The classical Sunnī position, transmitted across all four madhāhib, distinguishes tawassul from worship by the intention and the One actually being asked. The textual base is multiple: the ḥadīth of the blind man (recorded by al-Tirmidhī and others, with the duʿāʾ "*allāhumma innī asʾaluka wa-atawajjahu ilayka bi-Nabiyyika Muḥammad*"), and ʿUthmān ibn Ḥunayf's transmission of the same wording to a man with a need *after* the Prophet's ﷺ death (recorded by al-Ṭabarānī in *al-Muʿjam al-Kabīr*). Imam al-Subkī's *Shifāʾ al-Saqām* and Ibn Ḥajar al-Haytamī's *al-Jawhar al-Munaẓẓam* work through the categories in detail: the One asked is Allāh; the Prophet's ﷺ rank is the means (*wasīla*) by which Allāh is asked; the asking-by-rank no more constitutes worship of the Prophet ﷺ than asking-a-living-friend-for-duʿāʾ constitutes worship of the friend.

### Where the Salafī position runs into difficulty

The strongest difficulty is the ḥadīth of the blind man (al-Tirmidhī, *Sunan al-Tirmidhī* 3578, graded *ḥasan ṣaḥīḥ gharīb* by al-Tirmidhī himself).[^tirmidhi] The text contains the wording: *yā Muḥammad, innī atawajjahu bika ilā rabbī fī ḥājatī hādhihi li-tuqḍā lī* — "O Muḥammad, I turn through you to my Lord with this need of mine, that it might be granted." This is the precise form the Salafī position classifies as shirk in worship. The ḥadīth is not weak: al-Albānī himself authenticates it (in *Ṣaḥīḥ Sunan al-Tirmidhī* and again in his treatise *al-Tawassul: Anwāʿuhu wa-Aḥkāmuhu*).[^albani-tawassul] The Salafī position therefore has the wording it forbids and the source it accepts pointing in the same direction.

The second difficulty is the narration of ʿUthmān ibn Ḥunayf (al-Ṭabarānī, *al-Muʿjam al-Kabīr*)[^tabarani] — which records the same Companion teaching the same wording to a man with a need *after* the Prophet's ﷺ death. Al-Albānī authenticates the chain in *al-Tawassul* and then proposes that the wording's permitted scope is limited to the Prophet's ﷺ earthly life. The narration itself describes precisely the post-mortem case the qualification would exclude. A position whose conclusion contradicts the very narration it accepts as authentic is in difficulty on its own evidentiary terms.

[^tirmidhi]: Sunan al-Tirmidhī, no. 3578, "Kitāb al-Daʿawāt." Graded *ḥasan ṣaḥīḥ gharīb* by al-Tirmidhī. The ḥadīth is also recorded by Ibn Mājah (no. 1385) and authenticated by Ibn Khuzayma and al-Ḥākim, with al-Dhahabī concurring.

[^albani-tawassul]: Muḥammad Nāṣir al-Dīn al-Albānī, *al-Tawassul: Anwāʿuhu wa-Aḥkāmuhu* (Beirut: al-Maktab al-Islāmī, 1401/1980); idem, *Ṣaḥīḥ Sunan al-Tirmidhī* (Riyadh: Maktabat al-Maʿārif, 1997), vol. 3. Al-Albānī's authentication of the ḥadīth while simultaneously prohibiting the wording it contains is the central internal tension the classical Sunnī scholars identify in the Salafī treatment.

[^tabarani]: Al-Ṭabarānī, *al-Muʿjam al-Kabīr*, ḥadīth of ʿUthmān ibn Ḥunayf. The chain was examined and authenticated by al-Albānī in *al-Tawassul*, where he accepts its authenticity but argues the permissibility of the invocation was restricted to the Prophet's ﷺ lifetime — a qualification not found in the narration itself.

These two texts, accepted by the Salafī tradition as authentic, document exactly the practice the Salafī position prohibits. The classical Sunnī *uṣūl al-fiqh* framework — distinguishing the One actually asked (Allāh) from the *wasīla* through which He is asked (the Prophet's ﷺ rank) — preserves *tawḥīd* without requiring rejection of these texts.
