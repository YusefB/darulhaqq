### The Salafī position

The Salafī position is that binding taqlīd of any single madhhab is not an Islamic obligation and may in fact obstruct the correct practice of the religion. The starting point is a principle that commands broad agreement: when a ḥadīth is authentic and its meaning is clear, it takes priority over the opinion of any individual scholar, including the founders of the four madhāhib — all of whom are documented as having said something equivalent to "if the ḥadīth is authentic, that is my madhhab." The conclusion drawn is that the qualified Muslim should locate the strongest available evidence (*arjaḥ al-adilla*) on any given question directly from Qurʾān and Sunnah, understood as the Salaf understood them, rather than committing to the rulings of a single school regardless of the evidence. Islam Q&A states the principle: "What is obligatory is to follow that which is indicated by the evidence (*daleel*) of the Quran and Sunnah, even if it differs from what the madhhab says." The methodological move is a principled return to text over transmitted scholarly opinion.

### The classical Sunnī position

The classical Sunnī position is that taqlīd of one of the four recognized madhāhib — Ḥanafī, Mālikī, Shāfiʿī, or Ḥanbalī — is the default obligation for any Muslim who has not reached the level of a fully qualified mujtahid. The Qurʾānic basis is 16:43, "ask those who recall if you know not," which the scholars of uṣūl al-fiqh across all four schools take as establishing the obligation of taqlīd for the non-mujtahid. Shaykh Irshaad Sedick, citing Imam al-Būṭī's *al-Lā Madhhabiyya*, summarises: "Virtually all scholars of fundamentals of Islamic law have made this verse their principal evidence that the ordinary person (*non-mujtahid*) must follow the scholar who is an authoritative interpreter of the religious law of Islam (*mujtahid*)." The four-madhhab system is not, in the classical Sunnī framing, a later deviation from the Salaf: it is the institutional form in which the Salaf's transmitted knowledge was codified, preserved, and made operable across generations. To follow a madhhab is to follow the chain of transmission from the Prophet ﷺ through the Companions through the Tābiʿīn through the founding imams to the present — the same chain the Salafī tradition regards as authoritative in ḥadīth.

### Where the Salafī position runs into difficulty

The first and most structurally significant difficulty is that the Salafī position requires its practitioners to be mujtahids — or near-mujtahids — in order to execute it correctly. Determining which position is "strongest" on any given fiqh question requires competence in classical Arabic, mastery of the ḥadīth corpus and its grading criteria, knowledge of the transmitted *uṣūl* frameworks of all relevant schools, awareness of the prior scholarly literature on each question, and the capacity to weigh evidential conflicts in the manner the early Companions and their successors did. Imam al-Nawawī's introduction to *al-Majmūʿ*, and the uṣūl literature from al-Ghazālī's *al-Mustaṣfā* onward, specify these requirements precisely: the fully independent mujtahid is a distinct scholarly category, and the bar is set deliberately high. The classical Sunnī tradition's argument is not that the ḥadīth does not take priority — it does — but that knowing which ḥadīth applies to which case, in which way, under which conditions, and with which qualifications requires exactly the training the madhhab system exists to transmit. Imam al-Būṭī's *al-Lā Madhhabiyya* is the sustained argument for this point: abandoning the four-madhhab framework does not give access to the Qurʾān and Sunnah directly; it removes the institutional safeguards that make them operable as law.

The second difficulty is the selective application of the principle. The argument "follow the strongest evidence, not the madhhab" is applied in Salafī writing to the rulings of the four classical schools, but not to the methodological positions of contemporary Salafī scholars — whose rulings are transmitted, deferred to, and followed without the individual verification the principle would require. Shaykh al-Albānī's ḥadīth gradings, Shaykh Ibn Bāz's fatwas, Shaykh Ibn ʿUthaymīn's *al-Sharḥ al-Mumtiʿ* — these function, in practice, as the madhhab of the Salafī movement. Following them without independent verification is itself a form of taqlīd; calling it something else does not change its functional structure. The classical Sunnī position is not that taqlīd is intrinsically inferior to ijtihād — it is that taqlīd of verified, transmitted scholarship is the appropriate mode for those who have not themselves attained the qualifications of ijtihād, and that this description covers the overwhelming majority of those on both sides of this debate.

The third difficulty is historical. The Salafī position presents the four-madhhab framework as a later institutional development that introduced an intermediary between the Muslim and the text. But the four madhāhib are precisely the institutional form in which the knowledge of the Companions and Tābiʿīn was preserved, annotated, transmitted, and tested against subsequent cases across centuries of living scholarship. Al-Shāfiʿī's *al-Risāla*, Imam Mālik's *al-Muwaṭṭaʾ*, the recorded opinions of Imam Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal in *al-Masāʾil* — these are not departures from the Salaf but the primary vehicles through which the Salaf's practice is known. A position that distances itself from the four madhāhib in the name of returning to the Salaf is, on this reading, distancing itself from the most direct transmission of what the Salaf actually did.
