### The Salafī position

The Salafī movement permits the visiting of men's graves for the limited purpose of remembering death and the hereafter, citing the ḥadīth in *Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim* in which the Prophet ﷺ said: "I had forbidden you from visiting graves, but now visit them, for they remind you of the hereafter." Women's visiting of graves is generally restricted or prohibited, citing a separate ḥadīth in which the Prophet ﷺ "cursed the women who visit graves" — though Salafī scholars differ on whether this ḥadīth's prohibition is absolute or qualified by intent (lamentation, immodest dress, frequency).

The Salafī position sharply distinguishes this permitted "ziyārah sharʿiyyah" from a "ziyārah shirkiyyah" — visiting graves to ask the deceased for any form of help, intercession, or barakah, building structures or domes over graves, lighting lamps at them, or addressing the deceased with calls for aid. All of these latter practices are classed as either major shirk (taking the deceased as a deity) or as means leading to shirk that must be blocked under the principle of <em>sadd al-dharāʾiʿ</em>.

### The classical Sunnī position

The four madhāhib have historically held that visiting graves is recommended (<em>mustaḥabb</em>) for both men and women, with adab (decorum). Imam al-Nawawī (d. 676 AH) in *al-Adhkār* and *al-Majmūʿ* presents the consensus: men visit to remember death, to make duʿāʾ for the deceased, and to recite Qurʾān whose reward is sent to them; women visit under the same conditions, with the qualification that they not weep loudly or display immodest dress. The general permissibility-for-women is established from the ḥadīth of ʿĀʾisha visiting her brother's grave (narrated by al-Tirmidhī) and the Prophet's ﷺ visit to Baqīʿ in which he addressed the deceased: "<em>al-salāmu ʿalaykum dāra qawmin muʾminīn</em> — peace be upon you, abode of believers."

On asking Allāh through the rank of the righteous buried at a site (tawassul through them), the classical Sunnī position permits this and treats it as conceptually distinct from worship. The deceased is not the object of worship; Allāh alone is. The deceased is the <em>wasīla</em> — the means — by virtue of the rank Allāh has given them. Imam al-Subkī's *Shifāʾ al-Saqām* and Imam Aḥmad Zaynī Daḥlān's *al-Durar al-Saniyyah* work through the categories in detail; both are mainstream Sunnī mufti positions, not fringe.

### Where the Salafī position runs into difficulty

The Prophet ﷺ visited al-Baqīʿ and addressed the deceased buried there: *as-salāmu ʿalaykum dāra qawmin muʾminīn* — peace be upon you, abode of believers (*Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim*). This is a direct address to the dead in the second person, in a form Arabic grammar treats as direct speech. The Salafī position permits this specific instance but classifies the same syntactic form, when used at the graves of other deceased righteous people, as without basis. The asymmetric ruling — same form, different conclusion based on who is addressed — has not been textually justified.

The second difficulty: ʿĀʾisha (raḍiya Allāhu ʿanhā), umm al-muʾminīn, visited the grave of her brother ʿAbd al-Raḥmān, narrated by al-Tirmidhī. A general Salafī position that women's grave-visiting is prohibited or strongly discouraged sits in tension with the documented practice of one of the most authoritative women of the Salaf. The position's responses tend to qualify ʿĀʾisha's act as exceptional or as predating a later prohibition; neither qualification is textually documented.

The classical Sunnī position permits both — for men and women, at the Prophet's ﷺ grave and at the graves of the righteous — and treats the texts as the explicit basis for the permission rather than as exceptions to a presumed rule.
